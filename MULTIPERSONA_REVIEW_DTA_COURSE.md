# Multi-Persona Review: "When The Test Lies" DTA Course

---

## PERSONA 1: Dr. Sarah Chen
**Role:** Clinical Epidemiologist, Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health
**Experience:** 15 years in diagnostic test evaluation, Cochrane DTA author
**Perspective:** Methodological rigor and practical clinical application

### Strengths
- **Theranos hook is brilliant** - Every student knows this story, it creates immediate engagement
- **The 2x2 table presentation is clear** - Color-coded, memorable naming (TP/FP/FN/TN)
- **COVID rapid test data is current and accurate** - Dinnes et al. Cochrane review correctly cited
- **SnNout/SpPin mnemonics included** - These are clinical gold, students remember them for decades
- **Bivariate model explanation is accessible** - "You cannot pool sensitivity and specificity separately" is exactly right

### Concerns
- **Only ONE decision tree** - DTA is fundamentally about decision-making! Need many more:
  - Pre-test to post-test probability flow
  - Screening vs. diagnostic pathway
  - Sequential testing decision tree
  - Cost-consequence decision tree
- **Missing: Bayes' theorem visualization** - The base rate fallacy is THE most important concept
- **No Fagan nomogram** - This is standard DTA teaching, glaring omission
- **PSA screening controversy not included** - This is a MAJOR real-world story with data
- **No discussion of spectrum bias** - Critical concept for understanding why tests fail in practice

### Suggestions
1. Add at least 4-5 more decision trees throughout
2. Include Fagan nomogram with interactive pre-test probability
3. Add PSA screening story (US Preventive Services Task Force controversy)
4. Add HIV testing story (window period false negatives)
5. Include DES (diethylstilbestrol) pregnancy test tragedy

### Rating: 7.0/10
*"Good foundation, but needs more decision trees and the base rate fallacy module."*

## PERSONA 3: Professor Richard Deeks
**Role:** Biostatistician, University of Birmingham (namesake of Deeks' funnel plot)
**Experience:** 30 years in meta-analysis methods, developed key DTA statistics
**Perspective:** Statistical accuracy and methodological precision

### Strengths
- **Reitsma bivariate model correctly described** - The correlation explanation is accurate
- **HSROC mentioned appropriately** - Good that both models are acknowledged
- **I² interpretation thresholds are standard** - 25%/50%/75% cutoffs correctly stated
- **My funnel plot test is referenced** - Always nice to see proper attribution (Deeks et al. 2005)

### Concerns
- **No actual SROC curve shown** - You describe it but don't visualize it
- **Missing: confidence regions vs. prediction regions** - Critical distinction not explained
- **No discussion of QUADAS-2** - Quality assessment is essential for DTA reviews
- **Heterogeneity sources oversimplified** - Need more on threshold effect mathematics
- **No mention of verification bias** - This is the #1 bias in DTA studies
- **Publication bias section is missing entirely** - Just mentioned in passing

### Suggestions
1. Add actual SROC curve visualization (even stylized)
2. Include QUADAS-2 quality domains
3. Dedicate a section to biases in DTA studies
4. Show confidence vs. prediction ellipses on ROC space
5. Add publication bias module with Deeks' funnel plot interpretation

### Rating: 6.5/10
*"Pedagogically engaging but methodologically incomplete. Needs more statistical depth."*

## FINAL VERDICT

**Current Score: 6.7/10**

### Assessment

The course has **excellent storytelling and memorable teaching moments** but is **methodologically incomplete** and **lacks sufficient decision support tools**.

**Biggest Gaps:**
1. Only 1 decision tree (needs 5-10)
2. No Fagan nomogram or base rate visualization
3. Missing major real-world stories (PSA, HIV, TB, malaria)
4. No worked examples with calculations
5. QUADAS-2 and bias discussion absent

   - Pre-test to post-test probability
   - Screening vs. diagnostic pathway
